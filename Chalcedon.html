Chalcedon (451): Leo’s Tome, Canon 28, and the Clash of Primacies

  • Noel Bass
  • 28 May 2025

Historical Backdrop

The Council of Chalcedon was convened in 451 AD in response to the Christological controversy ignited by the Second Council of Ephesus (449), dubbed the “Robber Council” by Pope Leo I. The main goal was to settle the question of Christ's two natures — divine and human — in response to the teachings of Eutyches and Monophysitism. At the center of the controversy stood Pope Leo’s Tome, a letter sent to the council affirming the dual nature of Christ in perfect unity. Yet while the doctrinal issue was resolved with overwhelming consensus, a political and ecclesiological battle erupted over Canon 28, which sought to elevate Constantinople to near-equal status with Rome.

Leo’s Tome and “Peter Has Spoken”

Pope Leo I’s Tome was read at Chalcedon and met with acclaim. The bishops responded with the now-famous declaration:

“Peter has spoken through Leo!”

This phrase has been cited by Catholics as a confirmation of papal supremacy — that the pope speaks for the universal Church with Petrine authority. The Orthodox, however, interpret it differently: as a recognition of Leo’s correct doctrine, not his juridical supremacy. In this view, Leo’s words were accepted because they were true, not because he spoke ex cathedra.

Canon 28 and the Rise of Constantinople

After resolving the doctrinal disputes, the Council issued Canon 28, which stated:

“The bishops of Constantinople shall have equal privileges with those of old Rome, because it is the New Rome.”

This canon effectively gave Constantinople second place in the hierarchy — right after Rome — and implied a form of parity based on political stature. It bypassed Alexandria and Antioch, and it also challenged the idea of apostolic primacy being the basis of ecclesial authority.

Rome’s Rejection of Canon 28

Pope Leo rejected Canon 28 outright. He argued that ecclesiastical order was determined by apostolic foundation, not imperial politics. In his letter to Emperor Marcian, he wrote:

“This enactment was carried by the ambition of a few individuals and is opposed to the canons of Nicaea.”

Rome refused to ratify Canon 28, though it accepted the council’s Christological definition and canons otherwise. This marked a defining moment where Rome formally resisted conciliar innovation that threatened its primacy.

Did the Council Override Rome?

In a sense, yes — the council fathers passed Canon 28 even knowing Rome had not approved it. However, they still sent it to Leo for ratification, suggesting that his approval carried weight. The Eastern bishops later enforced Canon 28 despite Leo’s protests, indicating a divergence in understanding of authority: Rome saw primacy as jurisdictional and rooted in Peter; the East saw it as honorific and adaptable.

What Does Canon 28 Imply About Roman Authority?

Canon 28 reveals that by the mid-5th century, the East was no longer content with a purely Petrine understanding of Rome’s primacy. Authority was now being tied to imperial significance, not just apostolic foundation. This clashed directly with Rome’s self-understanding. If Rome’s primacy could be challenged by a canon from a council that Rome did not initiate and whose canon it refused, then the groundwork for the eventual schism was laid.

Conclusion

The Council of Chalcedon settled the Christological debates of its time but ignited new ecclesiological tensions. Pope Leo I was hailed as speaking with apostolic truth, but his rejection of Canon 28 was ultimately ignored by the East. This moment marks one of the clearest early examples of the clash between conciliarism and papal supremacy — and it remains a central fault line between Catholic and Orthodox understandings of Church authority.
